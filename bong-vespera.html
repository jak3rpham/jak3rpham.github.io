Why fictional
A real campaign requires audience testing, media planning, and stakeholder alignment that a solo experiment cannot produce. What this study demonstrates is the orchestration logic underneath — how a creative brief decomposes across the right AI models, how model boundaries get identified and routed around, and what shippable output looks like under credit constraint.

Most "AI in creative" demos use AI as a single tool — Midjourney here, ChatGPT there, finished in Photoshop. That demonstrates usage.

What I wanted to test: what does it look like when AI is treated as orchestration, not tool? What if every stage of a creative brief — concept expansion, atmospheric stills, typography, motion — gets handed to a different model based on what that model is genuinely good at?

The pipeline below is the answer I built for myself. Concept expansion goes to Claude. Atmospheric keyframes go to Flux Pro 1.1 Ultra. Vietnamese typography goes to GPT Image 1. Cinematic motion goes to Seedance 2.0.

No model does everything. The work is in matching them.

04 — Keyframes

Direction C, frame by frame

KF1 · The Gate at Dawn
Threshold

A weathered đền gate stands alone on a misty hillside at pre-dawn — entry point into a valley that remembers.

ModelFlux Pro 1.1 Ultra · 14 credits · 2 attempts
Iterationv1 rendered Japanese torii — wrong cultural register. v2 added "đầu đao curves, double-tier roof" + negative prompt "NOT Japanese torii". Architecture corrected.

Lesson: Vietnamese-specific architectural vocabulary is non-negotiable in prompts. Generic "Asian gate" defaults the model to torii every time.

KF2 · The Wanderer Enters
Solitary entry

A hooded traveler in deep teal robes and conical nón lá steps through the mist into the gate's opening.

ModelFlux Pro 1.1 Ultra · 7 credits · 1 attempt (single-shot)
Why it workedArchitectural vocabulary established in KF1 v2 transferred. Back-to-camera framing eliminated face-consistency burden.

Lesson: prompt vocabulary calibrated on one frame compounds — subsequent generations inherit the visual grammar without re-engineering.

KF3 · Path of Guardians
Watched

An overgrown stone path lined with weathered guardian statues, the wanderer small in mid-frame walking deeper.

ModelFlux Pro 1.1 Ultra · 7 credits · 1 attempt
Why it workedSpecific statue variety (kneeling, headless, eroded, mossy) gave the model anchor points instead of uniform rows.

Lesson: when generating multiple of the same object class, specifying internal variation prevents the model from defaulting to identical replicas.

KF4 · The Kneeling Moment
Tenderness, before

The wanderer's gloved hand brushes moss from a fallen statue's serene closed-eye face. A vermillion leaf rests nearby.

ModelFlux Pro 1.1 Ultra · 7 credits · 1 attempt (accepted with compromises)
CompromiseStatue rendered as Buddha (urna gem, curled hair) instead of Vietnamese folk deity. Hand anatomy correct (5 fingers).

Lesson: Flux has a strong Buddha prior on "stone deity statue." Aggressive negative prompts (NOT Buddha, NOT urna mark, NOT curled hair) would override — but credits were conserved for the harder KF5.

KF5 · After the Recognition
The pivot — hero shot

The wanderer stands frozen, head tilted toward distant ember points awakening in the fog. Three pinpricks of vermillion in the deep distance — implication, not spectacle.

ModelFlux Pro 1.1 Ultra · 21 credits · 3 attempts (2 failed, 1 success after pivot)
FailureAttempts 1+2 prompted "subtle red eye glow" → both rendered demonic horror eyes. Model has trained prior coupling stone statue + glowing eye → demon. Cannot override via prompt.
PivotReframed entire shot to show implication, not literal reveal. Wanderer reacting to distant ember points instead of statue's eye opening.

Lesson: the implication landed harder than the literal would have. Identifying a model boundary, accepting it, and finding a creative path around it under credit constraint — that is the senior signal.

06 — Motion

Two video samples, intentionally

Both included to show the boundary between free tier capability and production capability. The contrast is the lesson.

Free tier · Multiframes · 720p
Chained 5-frame transition

All 5 keyframes fed sequentially into Dreamina Multiframes. Output is visually inconsistent — "ảo đá" — because the scene gaps between frames exceed the model's coherence threshold. Included deliberately. It shows where the boundary is.

Seedance 2.0 · 1080p · 485 credits
KF3 → KF5 cinematic transition

Two coherent frames (forest path → wide reveal) interpolated via Seedance 2.0 image-to-video. Smooth dolly-out, mist drift, ember pulse intact. This is what production-tier motion looks like when the input frames share visual language.

The lesson: Multiframes free tier is the wrong tool for chained narrative. Pair-wise generation across coherent frame couples + manual timeline assembly in CapCut is the production workflow. Free tier reveals the architecture; paid tier executes it.

08 — Learnings

What I learned

i
Pivot under constraint

KF5 — the payoff frame — failed twice in a row. Both Flux attempts produced demonic horror eyes when prompted for a stone statue with subtle red glow. The fault wasn't prompt engineering. It was Flux's trained prior coupling stone statue + glowing eye → demon.

The fix wasn't a better prompt. The fix was reframing the entire shot — instead of showing the eye opening literally, I rebuilt the frame to show the wanderer reacting to distant embers awakening in the fog. The implication landed harder than the literal would have.

The pivot is the senior signal. Identifying a model boundary, accepting it, and finding a creative path around it under credit constraint.

ii
Free tier as transparency

Two video samples are intentionally included in this demo. The Multiframes "ảo đá" output is not a bug to apologise for — it's evidence of where the free tier breaks. The Seedance 2.0 single-frame is what the same project looks like when the right tool meets a constrained problem.

Hiding the failure would have been easier. Showing it, with the diagnosis written underneath, is what distinguishes a portfolio piece from a marketing brochure.

iii
Process > output

This is not a finished campaign. There is no media plan, no audience testing, no production budget context. The CTA layer was dropped. The hook copy was deferred to caption text rather than baked into the image.

What this proves: I can take a creative brief, decompose it across the right AI models, identify and route around model limitations, and ship a coherent visual deliverable in a single workday — including the transparency to flag what didn't work.

The orchestration is the work.
